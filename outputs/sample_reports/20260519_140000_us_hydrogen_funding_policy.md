## Executive Summary
The US hydrogen sector entered 2026 with significant federal funding still flowing through the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) and Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (BIL), though the legislative environment has introduced new uncertainties. Seven Regional Clean Hydrogen Hubs (H2Hubs) selected in 2023 are progressing through NEPA review and preliminary engineering, with first production milestones expected no earlier than 2027. The 45V clean hydrogen production tax credit remains the most consequential policy instrument: proposed Treasury guidance on well-to-gate emissions accounting has narrowed the qualifying pathway, creating significant investor uncertainty for electrolytic hydrogen projects tied to the grid. The DOE Loan Programs Office (LPO) has emerged as a critical funding bridge for large projects unable to close project finance under current market conditions. US electrolyzer manufacturers — led by Plug Power, Nel, and Ohmium — continue to struggle with profitability despite growing demand signals. Strategic implication: the IRA fiscal framework remains intact as of Q2 2026, but regulatory implementation risk is high. Projects that have not yet achieved conditional DOE loan commitments or secured IRS pre-approval letters face meaningful execution risk as the 2026 election cycle approaches.

## Key Developments
- **45V guidance finalized (partial).** Treasury published final guidance for the 45V clean hydrogen credit in March 2026, adopting an additionality, deliverability, and temporal matching framework. Only projects demonstrating hourly matching of renewable electricity will qualify for the maximum $3/kg credit. Strategic implication: this severely constrains the project economics for grid-connected electrolyzers; behind-the-meter renewable PPAs become structurally preferred.
- **H2Hub progress divergence.** Of the seven selected H2Hubs, three (PNW, Appalachian, and Gulf Coast) have cleared NEPA; four remain in environmental review with timelines extending to late 2026. Strategic implication: hub deployment is running 12–18 months behind original schedule, delaying anchor demand signals for hydrogen supply chains.

Strategic implication: DOE LPO is functioning as a de facto lender of last resort for commercially borderline projects — signalling both policy commitment and private capital caution.

Outcome uncertain. Strategic implication: regulatory uncertainty will persist through the election; investors are pricing in a 12–24 month policy ambiguity window.
- **DOE 1 MT Hydrogen Shot update.** Progress toward the $1/kg by 2031 target is tracking behind schedule; DOE acknowledged current electrolyzer cost reductions are proceeding at approximately half the target pace.

## Market Implications
The US hydrogen market is in a critical transition: federal policy architecture is largely in place, but implementation frictions — regulatory complexity, permitting timelines, and private capital caution — are delaying commercialisation. The market bifurcation between industrial hydrogen (existing grey hydrogen demand) and green electrolytic hydrogen is sharpening.

Industrial hydrogen users in refining, ammonia, and methanol remain primarily cost-driven. Green hydrogen at $3–5/kg is not competitive with grey hydrogen at $1.50–2/kg absent the full 45V credit stack. The regulatory uncertainty around 45V is therefore directly suppressing investment decisions in projects that cannot demonstrate hourly matching.

The DOE LPO's role has expanded de facto into project finance, filling a gap left by private lenders unwilling to take first-of-kind technology risk. This is historically consistent with energy infrastructure build-out patterns (nuclear, offshore wind) but creates concentration risk in federal balance sheets.

## Risks and Watchouts
- **Election cycle policy risk.** The 2026 midterm elections and 2028 presidential cycle introduce meaningful risk of IRA modification. Republican proposals to simplify or eliminate 45V additionality requirements could reshape project economics in either direction.
- **45V regulatory complexity creating investment paralysis.** The hourly matching requirement is technically and administratively burdensome. Many projects are deferring FID until IRS pre-approval processes are better defined.
