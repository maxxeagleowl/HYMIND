## Market Implications
The European electrolyzer market is transitioning from pilot-scale to commercial deployment, but cost economics remain challenging. Green hydrogen production at competitive prices requires either significant electrolyzer cost reductions (to below 400 EUR/kW installed) or higher carbon prices that shift the relative economics of fossil alternatives.

The demand side is increasingly bifurcated: mobility and heating applications have stalled, while industrial applications in ammonia, steel, and refining are driving the bulk of new offtake contracts. This concentration creates single-sector dependency risk for the market.

Competition from US IRA-subsidised production and anticipated Australian and Chilean export capacity is reshaping European hydrogen import strategies. The 2026 targets set under REPowerEU (10 Mt of domestic production and 10 Mt of imports by 2030) appear achievable only under optimistic scenarios given current project FID rates.

## Risks and Watchouts
- **Permitting delays remain the single largest bottleneck.** Average permitting timeline for large-scale electrolysis facilities in Germany is 4.2 years. Without regulatory streamlining, 2030 targets are unreachable.
- **Commodity price exposure for platinum group metals.** PEM electrolyzers require iridium (catalyst). Iridium spot prices increased 18% YTD due to South African supply constraints. This adds approximately €30–50/kW to PEM stack costs.
- **Offtake contract bankability.** Many project financings remain unclosed due to lender reluctance to accept offtake counterparties without investment-grade credit ratings. This constrains project pipeline conversion rates.
- **Grid connection constraints.** Renewable electricity connection queues in Germany, Poland, and France are extending project timelines by 18–24 months on average.
- **Single-sector concentration risk.** Over 60% of European electrolyzer offtake is tied to ammonia and refinery applications. A slowdown in industrial decarbonisation mandates would significantly reduce near-term demand.
